## The Discovery

After root-position analysis scored all 22 letters across R1, R2, and R3 radical positions, three letters came back "position-dependent" with low consistency: Gimel, Tet, and Qof. Rather than treating these as failures, Daniel recognized them — and three other letters — as the **6Q questions** that structure relational space.

The 6Q framework (Who, What, How, Where, When, Why) maps onto six Hebrew letters. These letters don't describe a single relational dynamic because **they are the questions themselves** — and questions shift meaning by context. The remaining 16 letters are the substance that answers them.

## Structural Observations

### 1. Position in the Alphabet

The six 6Q letters span the alphabet: **1, 2, 3, 4, 19, 22**.

The first four are sequential — Who (א), What (ב), How (ג), Where (ד). These are the foundational questions that establish a scene. You must know the agent, the form, the process, and the location before deeper questions become possible.

Qof (19) and Tav (22) appear near the end. When and Why are the temporal and purposive questions that require the earlier four to already be in play. You cannot ask "when" or "why" until you know who, what, how, and where.

### 2. The "Position-Dependent" Letters Explained

Root-position analysis found three letters with low consistency: Gimel, Tet, and Qof. Two of these (Gimel = How, Qof = When) are 6Q questions. Their inconsistency across root positions is not a deficiency — it is a faithful reflection of their nature as **context-sensitive questions**. The answer to "how" depends on where you are in the process. The answer to "when" depends on the frame of reference.

Tet (the third "position-dependent" letter) is not mapped to a 6Q question here. Its dynamic — materializing (R1), stretching (R2), compacting (R3) — describes a spatial-material lifecycle. It may serve a different structural role yet to be identified.

### 3. Skeleton and Flesh

The 6Q letters form a **skeleton** — the structural questions that define relational space. The remaining 16 letters are the **flesh** — the dynamics that answer those questions.

- Aleph asks "Who?" → Yud (focused agency) provides an answer
- Bet asks "What?" → Chet (vitalization), Kaf (bounded actualization), Samekh (encirclement) provide different kinds of form
- Gimel asks "How?" → the process letters describe different methods of traversal
- Dalet asks "Where?" → Lamed (direction toward) provides spatial orientation
- Qof asks "When?" → temporal dynamics locate events in the continuum
- Tav asks "Why?" → completion and purpose seal the journey

This skeleton-flesh distinction implies that the 22 letters are not a flat list of equal dynamics but a **two-layer architecture**: six questions that structure the space, and sixteen dynamics that fill it.

## Theological Implications

The 6Q mapping connects to the emanation principle established in the relational synthesis: the signs point to the highest forms that manifest from God, not to God directly.

If six letters ARE the fundamental questions — the structure of inquiry itself — then the alphabet begins with an implicit claim: **reality is structured by relationship**. Who, What, How, Where, When, Why are not human inventions imposed on a formless world. They are the joints of creation. The alphabet encodes them first (1-4) and last (19, 22), framing everything between.

The silent Aleph as "Who" is particularly resonant. The first question is about the subject — and the first letter is silent. The originator is present but not heard. You know Who by what follows.
